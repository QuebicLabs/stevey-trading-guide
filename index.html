What is trading?
Week 1: Absolute Basics · 30 min
▼

The 30-second version

Trading is buying something at one price and selling it at another. If you buy low and sell high, you make money. If you buy high and sell low, you lose money. That's it. Everything else is details.

What we trade: futures contracts

You're not going to trade stocks (Apple, Tesla, etc.) in this course. You're going to trade futures, specifically a futures contract called MNQ (Micro E-mini Nasdaq-100).

What's a futures contract?

It's a deal between two people that says "I'll buy this thing from you at this price on this date." Hundreds of years ago, farmers used futures to lock in the price of corn before harvest. Today, traders use futures to bet on whether prices will go up or down.

Why MNQ specifically?

  • It's small. The "Micro" in MNQ means it's 1/10th the size of the regular contract. Small = less risk per trade.
  • It's liquid. Lots of traders are always buying and selling it, so you can always get in and out fast.
  • It moves predictably. The Nasdaq-100 (which MNQ tracks) is one of the cleanest charts in trading.
  • It's open 23 hours a day. You can trade it whenever fits your schedule.

Long vs Short

Two ways to make money:

  • Going LONG (buying): You think price will go UP. You buy now, sell later at a higher price. Profit = sell price − buy price.
  • Going SHORT (selling): You think price will go DOWN. You sell now, buy back later at a lower price. Profit = sell price − buy price (still positive because sell was higher).

Don't worry about how shorting works mechanically yet. Just know you can make money in both directions. That's a superpower compared to old-school stock investors who can only profit when prices go up.

Reading a candlestick
The most important visual in trading · 30 min
▼

What is a candlestick?

A candlestick is a single rectangle (sometimes with lines sticking out the top and bottom) that shows you what happened to price during a specific time period. One candle = one time period.

If you're on a 5-minute chart, each candle = 5 minutes. On a 1-hour chart, each candle = 1 hour. On a 1-day chart, each candle = 1 day.

Anatomy of a candle

The 4 numbers that matter

Every candle has exactly four data points (called OHLC):

  • O = OPEN — The price when the time period started
  • H = HIGH — The highest price reached during the period
  • L = LOW — The lowest price reached
  • C = CLOSE — The price when the time period ended

Color rules

  • Green (or hollow): Close was HIGHER than open. Bulls won this candle.
  • Red (or filled): Close was LOWER than open. Bears won.

What candles tell you

Once you can read a single candle, you can read a whole chart. A series of candles tells you a story: who's winning, who's losing, where the action is.

Practice tip: Open TradingView (after Day 5), pick any chart, and just LOOK at the candles. Don't trade. Just watch a green candle form, then a red one. Get used to seeing them. The patterns will start jumping out.

The same chart, different zooms

The same market — say, MNQ — looks completely different depending on what time frame you're looking at. Think of it like a map: a world map shows continents, a city map shows streets, a building floor plan shows rooms. It's all the same place. Just different zoom levels.

Common time frames

The "top down" approach

Pro traders look at multiple time frames in order, biggest to smallest:

  1. Start with the 1-hour or 4-hour — what's the big picture? Is the market trending up, down, or sideways?
  2. Drop to the 15-minute — where are the key levels and zones today?
  3. Drop to the 5-minute — find your exact entry trigger

This is called "top-down analysis." It keeps you from making dumb trades against the bigger trend.

Rule of thumb: Trade WITH the higher time frame, not against it. If the 1-hour says "going down," you should be looking for shorts, not longs, on the 5-minute.

What makes price move?
Buyers, sellers, and supply/demand · 25 min
▼

The simplest possible explanation

Price goes up when more people want to BUY than SELL. Price goes down when more people want to SELL than BUY. That's the entire mechanism.

Who's actually trading?

  • Banks and hedge funds — by far the biggest movers. Trade in millions of dollars per click.
  • Algorithmic traders — computer programs that buy/sell based on pre-set rules. Most volume on any given day.
  • Retail traders — that's us. Individual humans clicking buttons. We're tiny in the grand scheme.

Our edge as small retail traders isn't size or speed — it's flexibility. We can sit out 95% of the day and only trade when the setup is screaming at us. Big institutions HAVE to trade. We don't.

What moves MNQ specifically

  • Big tech earnings (Apple, Microsoft, Nvidia, Google, Amazon, Meta, Tesla) — these companies dominate the Nasdaq-100
  • Federal Reserve announcements (FOMC meetings, every 6 weeks) — interest rate decisions move EVERYTHING
  • Economic data (CPI inflation, jobs reports) — released at 8:30 AM ET on schedule
  • Pre-market news — anything that happens before 9:30 AM ET cash open
  • Random Tuesday — sometimes the market just moves and nobody knows why
Don't trade during news events. When CPI or FOMC drops, price can move 50-100 points in 5 seconds. That's enough to blow through any stop loss you set. Sit out and watch.

Why futures, not stocks?
Week 2: Futures & MNQ · 25 min

The big differences

StocksFutures (MNQ)

The key advantage: PDT rule does not apply

Stocks have something called the Pattern Day Trader (PDT) rule. If you have less than $25,000 in your stock account and you make 4+ day trades in 5 business days, your broker locks your account. Futures don't have this rule. You can day trade with a $500 account if you want.

The trade-off: leverage cuts both ways

One MNQ contract controls $50,000+ worth of Nasdaq stocks with only ~$50 of margin. That's amazing on the way up — and brutal on the way down. This is why risk management is the entire game, not picking direction.

Leverage can blow your account in 5 minutes. A 50-point move on MNQ is $100. A 250-point move is $500. We've seen 250-point moves in 10 minutes during news events. If you don't have a stop loss, you can lose your entire account on one bad trade. We'll cover stops on Day 12.

Minis vs Micros · The math you MUST know

The two sizes of Nasdaq futures

The CME (Chicago Mercantile Exchange) offers two versions of the Nasdaq-100 future:

SymbolNamePer pointPer tickTick size
NQE-mini Nasdaq$20$50.25 pts
MNQMicro E-mini Nasdaq$2$0.500.25 pts

MNQ is exactly 1/10th the size of NQ. Same chart. Same price. Same hours. Just less money on the line per point.

The math you NEED to memorize

For MNQ:
  • 1 point = $2 per contract
  • 1 tick = $0.50 per contract (4 ticks per point)
  • 10 points = $20 per contract
  • 50 points = $100 per contract
  • 100 points = $200 per contract

Real example

Let's say MNQ is trading at 25,800. You go LONG (buy) 1 contract.

  • If price goes to 25,810 (+10 points) → +$20
  • If price goes to 25,830 (+30 points) → +$60
  • If price goes to 25,750 (−50 points) → −$100

Why we use MNQ as beginners

The dollar math is small enough that mistakes don't ruin you. A 50-point loss on MNQ = $100. A 50-point loss on NQ = $1,000. We'd rather you learn with $100 mistakes, not $1,000 ones.

Pro move: Once you're consistently profitable on 1 MNQ contract, the next step isn't to jump to NQ. The next step is to trade 2 MNQ. Then 3. Scale gradually. There's no medal for using the bigger contract.

MNQ trades almost 24 hours, but volume is concentrated

The market is technically open Sunday 6 PM ET through Friday 5 PM ET (closed for an hour each day). But not all hours are equal. Volume — and therefore opportunity and risk — is concentrated in three main sessions:

Bid, ask, spread, and slippage
The hidden costs of trading · 25 min
▼

Every market has TWO prices

When you look at a quote, you don't see one price — you see two:

  • BID — The highest price someone is willing to buy at right now
  • ASK (or "offer") — The lowest price someone is willing to sell at right now

The difference between them is the spread. On MNQ, the spread is usually 1 tick ($0.50). That means every time you enter a trade, you're already starting at a $0.50 loss.

How orders fill

  • If you BUY at market, you pay the ASK (the higher price)
  • If you SELL at market, you receive the BID (the lower price)
  • You can also place a LIMIT order — "I'll only buy if price comes down to X" — but it might not fill

Slippage

Slippage is when the price moves between when you click the button and when the order actually fills. On MNQ, slippage is usually 0-1 tick during normal hours and 5-20+ ticks during news events. This is another reason not to trade during news.

Stops can slip too. If you set a stop loss at 25,750 and there's a fast move, you might get filled at 25,745 or worse. This is why we don't trade right before economic data drops — slippage on stops can be brutal.

The 1% rule (this is the most important lesson)
Week 3: Risk Management · 30 min
▼

The single rule that keeps you in the game

NEVER risk more than 1% of your account on a single trade.

Read it again. Memorize it. This rule is more important than any setup, any indicator, any strategy you'll learn.

Why 1%?

Because you WILL have losing streaks. Even great traders lose 5 trades in a row sometimes. Math:

Risk per trade10 losses in a row20 losses in a row
1%−10% (recoverable)−18% (annoying but fine)
2%−18%−33%
5%−40%−64%
10%−65%−88% (account dead)

What does 1% look like in dollars?

  • $500 account: $5 per trade
  • $1,000 account: $10 per trade
  • $5,000 account: $50 per trade
  • $10,000 account: $100 per trade
  • $25,000 account: $250 per trade (this is the prop firm size)

How this controls position size

Your stop loss distance × MNQ point value × number of contracts = total risk.

Example: $5,000 account, 1% rule = $50 max risk. If your stop is 25 points away from entry, that's 25 × $2 = $50 per contract. So you can trade 1 MNQ. If your stop is 50 points away, that's $100 per contract — you can't even trade 1 contract without breaking the rule. Either tighten the stop or skip the trade.

The killer mistake: "I'll just use a wider stop this time, it's a special setup." NO. Stop = 1% of account. Always. If the setup needs a wider stop, you don't take it. Period.

Stop losses · what, why, how
The single most important order type · 30 min
▼

What is a stop loss?

A stop loss is an order you place at the same time as your trade that says "if price moves against me to THIS level, sell automatically." It's the brake on your car.

Why every trade needs one

  1. You can't watch the screen 100% of the time. Network drops, you go pee, the cat steps on the keyboard. Stops protect you when you can't react.
  2. Emotions lie. When a trade goes against you, your brain will say "it'll come back." It might not. Stops execute without asking your brain.
  3. It defines your risk before you enter. Without a stop, your max loss is "all your money." With a stop, your max loss is a number you chose.

How to place a stop

For a LONG trade, your stop goes BELOW your entry. For a SHORT trade, your stop goes ABOVE.

The 4 types of stops

  • Hard stop: Order placed with the broker. Executes automatically. Always use these.
  • Mental stop: "I'll exit when price hits X." Don't do this. You will not exit.
  • Trailing stop: Stop that moves with you as price moves in your favor. Locks in profits.
  • Time stop: "If this trade isn't working in 20 minutes, I exit." Useful for fades.

Take profit zones
Where to lock in winners · 25 min
▼

The opposite of a stop

A take profit (TP) is an order that automatically closes your trade when price reaches a level in your favor. It locks in your win.

Where to put your TP

  1. At a known level — previous day high/low, session high/low, a Fair Value Gap, an Order Block. These are places price is likely to react.
  2. At a fixed multiple of your stop — if your stop is 25 points, your TP at 1R = 25 points, 2R = 50 points, 3R = 75 points.
  3. At round numbers — 25,800 / 25,900 / 26,000. These act as magnets and rejection zones.

The "scaling out" strategy

Instead of one big TP, take partial profits at multiple levels:

  • 1R: Sell HALF your contracts. Move stop to breakeven (your entry price). Now you have a "free trade" — can't lose money.
  • 2R: Sell another quarter. Trail your stop closer.
  • 3R+: Let the rest run with a trailing stop.
This is how the big winners come. The trader who exits at 1R every time will have a flat P&L. The trader who takes some at 1R, some at 2R, and lets a runner go to 5R is the one who actually makes money.

Risk-to-reward (RR) ratios
Why you can be wrong half the time and still make money · 25 min
▼

The math that changes everything

R:R = how many points you stand to GAIN vs how many points you stand to LOSE on a trade.

The magic of asymmetric bets

If you risk $50 to make $100 (1:2 R:R) and you're right 50% of the time:

  • 10 trades: 5 wins × $100 = +$500
  • 5 losses × $50 = −$250
  • Net: +$250

If you risk $50 to make $50 (1:1 R:R) and you're right 50% of the time, you break even (minus fees, you lose).

The rule: Don't take any trade that's worse than 1:1.5 R:R. Aim for 1:2 minimum. The best setups give you 1:3 or better.

Position sizing calculator

How many contracts should you trade?

The answer depends on your account size, your risk tolerance, and where your stop is. Use the calculator in the Tools section below for every single trade.

The formula

Max contracts = (account × risk%) ÷ (stop distance × point value)

Example 1

  • Account: $5,000
  • Risk %: 1% = $50
  • Stop distance: 25 points
  • Point value (MNQ): $2
  • $50 ÷ (25 × $2) = $50 ÷ $50 = 1 contract

Example 2

  • Account: $10,000
  • Risk %: 1% = $100
  • Stop distance: 20 points
  • $100 ÷ (20 × $2) = $100 ÷ $40 = 2 contracts

Example 3 (DON'T)

  • Account: $5,000
  • Risk %: 1% = $50
  • Stop distance: 50 points
  • $50 ÷ (50 × $2) = $50 ÷ $100 = 0.5 contracts
  • You can't trade half a contract. Either skip the trade or tighten the stop.

Intro to Smart Money Concepts (SMC)
Week 4: SMC & Charting · 30 min
▼

What is SMC?

Smart Money Concepts is a trading methodology built on the idea that big institutions (banks, hedge funds) leave footprints on the chart when they enter and exit large positions. If you can spot those footprints, you can trade in the same direction as the people moving the market.

The footprints we look for

  • Fair Value Gaps (FVGs) — Day 17. A gap left when price moves too fast.
  • Order Blocks (OBs) — Day 18. The last opposing candle before a big move.
  • Liquidity sweeps — Wicks that take out swing highs/lows to grab stops.
  • Session levels — Day 19. Asian/London/NY ranges that act as magnets.

Why does SMC work?

It works because markets are governed by liquidity. When a big institution wants to buy 10,000 contracts, they can't just click "buy 10,000" — that would push price way up and they'd pay a terrible average. Instead, they:

  1. Wait for retail traders (us) to put their stops in obvious places (above swing highs)
  2. Push price into those stops, triggering them — this creates a flood of sell orders
  3. The institution buys all those sell orders quietly at a discount
  4. NOW they can let price run higher, with their position already loaded

This is why "the obvious trade" usually fails first, then works. SMC lets you see this pattern coming.

Fair Value Gaps (FVG) · the core concept
Your indicator highlights these automatically · 35 min
▼

What is an FVG?

A Fair Value Gap is a 3-candle pattern where price moved so fast that it left an imbalance — a zone where buyers and sellers didn't have time to transact at fair value.

Bullish FVG

The low of candle 3 is above the high of candle 1. Big gap up. Price often comes back to "fill" this gap before continuing higher.

Bearish FVG

The high of candle 3 is below the low of candle 1. Big gap down. Price often returns to fill before continuing lower.

How to use FVGs in trades

  1. The QL SMC indicator marks unfilled FVGs as colored boxes on your chart
  2. Wait for price to come BACK to the FVG (not break through, just touch the edge)
  3. Look for a reversal candle inside the FVG — wick rejection, engulfing pattern, bullish/bearish close
  4. Enter in the direction of the original FVG move
  5. Stop loss just outside the opposite edge of the FVG
  6. Target: the next FVG in the direction of the trade, or PDH/PDL

HTF FVGs are stronger than LTF FVGs

A 4-hour FVG is much more powerful than a 5-minute FVG. Why? Because it took longer to form, more traders saw it, more orders are stacked at that level. Your indicator shows HTF FVGs as the bigger, more solid colored boxes.

Stevey rule: Only trade FVGs from 15-minute or higher time frames during your first 30 days. Skip the 5m and 3m FVGs. They're too noisy and you'll get chopped up.

Order Blocks
The footprint of institutional orders · 25 min
▼

What is an Order Block?

An Order Block is the last opposing candle before a strong directional move. The theory: that's where institutions placed their large orders, which is why the market reversed/broke out from there.

The 2 types

  • Bullish OB (blue boxes): The last RED (down) candle before a strong move UP that breaks a recent swing high. When price returns to this candle's range, expect a bounce.
  • Bearish OB (purple boxes): The last GREEN (up) candle before a strong move DOWN that breaks a recent swing low. When price returns to this candle's range, expect rejection.

OB + FVG combo = highest conviction

When an Order Block AND a Fair Value Gap overlap at the same price level, you have the strongest possible setup. Why? Because two independent SMC concepts are pointing at the same zone. Trade these aggressively.

  3. Are there unfilled HTF FVGs nearby? Those are your magnet zones.
  4. Is price near PDH/PDL/PWH/PWL? Watch for sweeps + reversals.
  5. Does the setup line up? Killzone + bias + FVG/OB at level = trade.

The Asian Session Strategy (Stevey's bread & butter)
Week 5: Setups & Execution · 35 min
▼

The setup

Asian session is range-bound 80% of the time. We exploit the boundaries.

Entry rules (Bullish version)

  1. Wait until 9 PM ET (1 hour into Asian session) so the range is somewhat established
  2. Identify the Asian session high and low
  3. Watch for price to drop near the Asian session LOW (within ~10 points)
  4. Look for a bullish FVG forming on the 5m chart inside that zone
  5. Wait for a bullish reversal candle (long lower wick rejecting, or bullish engulfing)
  6. Enter LONG on the close of the reversal candle
  7. Stop loss: 5 points below the Asian session low
  8. Target: Middle of the Asian range (1R), then full Asian high (2R+)

Entry rules (Bearish version)

Same thing, mirrored. Wait for price near Asian high, look for bearish FVG, bearish reversal candle, enter SHORT, stop above Asian high, target Asian middle then low.

When NOT to take this trade

  • If there's a strong trend already in place — Asian range is broken, this is a breakout day, not a fade day
  • If price has already swept the level and reversed — you're late
  • If you're not in the Asian killzone window
  • If the spread is wide or volume is unusually high (news happening)

Before EVERY trade, run this checklist

  1. ☐ Is it inside a killzone? (orange tint visible)

Trading psychology · why your brain is your worst enemy
Week 6: Psychology & Prop Firms · 30 min
▼

The 4 emotions that destroy traders

  1. Fear of missing out (FOMO) — "I see a move happening, I have to get in." This is how you enter at the worst possible price.
  2. Revenge trading — "I just lost $50, I need to make it back." This is how $50 becomes $500.
  3. Hope — "It'll come back, I'll just wait." Hope is not a strategy. Hope is what kills accounts.
  4. Greed — "I'm up $80, let me hold for $200." This is how winners turn into losers.

The 4 habits that save you from yourself

  1. Walk away after entry. Set your stop and target, then literally close the chart for 5 minutes.
  2. One trade per day. If you take one, you're done. Win or lose. This kills FOMO and revenge.
  3. Journal every trade. Writing it down makes you confront your emotions.
  4. Have a hard daily loss limit. If you lose 2% of your account in a day, log out for 24 hours.
The truth: Most traders blow up not because their strategy is bad, but because they can't follow rules when emotions hit. The strategy in this course is good. Whether you make money depends on whether you can resist your own brain.
27
Letting trades play out
Patience is the hardest skill · 25 min
▼

The exit obsession

New traders watch every tick after entry. They exit at 1R because they're scared. They exit at -5 points because they "feel" it's wrong. They move stops because they panic. This is the #1 killer of paper-profitable traders going live.

The 3 exit rules

  1. Set your stop and target before entry. Don't change them after.
  2. Walk away from the screen for 5 minutes after entry. Set a phone timer. Come back when it goes off.
  3. The only valid mid-trade adjustment is moving stop to breakeven AFTER 1R is hit. Never widen a stop. Never.

The "watch the timeframe" rule

Trading off a 5m chart? Don't watch the 1m. Trading off the 15m? Don't watch the 5m. Watching a smaller timeframe than you traded on will make you panic over noise that doesn't matter.

How prop firms work
Trading other people's money · 30 min
▼

What is a prop firm?

A "proprietary trading firm" gives you capital to trade with. You don't risk your own money — you risk theirs. In exchange, they take a cut of profits (usually 10-20%). You keep 80-90%.

The two-step process

  1. Evaluation: You pay an upfront fee (~$100-300) to take an "eval" account. You have to hit a profit target without violating any rules. If you pass, you get a funded account.
  2. Funded account: Now you trade with real firm money. You keep 80-90% of profits. The firm covers losses (up to a limit).

The rules that make you bust

  • Profit target: Hit X% profit to pass
  • Daily loss limit: Lose more than X% in one day = busted
  • Max drawdown (often trailing): Total losses can't exceed Y% from peak balance
  • Min trading days: Must trade at least N days to pass
  • Consistency rule: No single day can be more than 30-40% of total profit
  • News restrictions: Many firms ban trading 2 min around major news

Why the trailing drawdown is the killer

If you start at $25,000 with a $1,500 trailing drawdown, your floor starts at $23,500. But every time you make new highs, the floor moves up. You can be net positive overall and still bust because the drawdown trailed your peak. This is why prop firm traders care more about avoiding losses than maximizing wins.
